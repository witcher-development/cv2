This is an attempt to challenge the conventional way of doing CV

Hiring is a bit of a gamble for both sides. Let me try to make it less of a gamble for you.

Here is the list of risks I'd be considering as a hiring person and I guess you do too.

Let me answer them for you.

  1. Will he manage this job?

    Most programming isn't rocket science. The hard part is communication and collaboration. You have a gap to close - I'll learn your approach, problems and the domain, and help you move faster.

    On my last job I landed as frontend developer into a startup, but ended up:

    • Fixing developer exprience and build times.
    • Conducting the process of creating and enforcing conventions for the new code. With each task-related PR I migrated a part of old code to match those conventions too.
    • In the absense of the team lead I took charge at a team mate's critical situation with a delivery of a time-pressured feature. I identified misalignments between the requirements and implementation, found a temporary technical mitigation, fixed communication and documented everything to get team lead up to speed with the sutiation.
    • Managed navigating in an unkown domain (cybersecurity) and producing code that reflects this domain well.

  2. Will he bring something new to the table?

    I have an active life, many interests and 8 years of experience in the field behind me. There is a big chance that my background + your background would be a cool combo.

  4. How long will it take for him to get up to speed?

    Last time it took me 1 week to figure out the main things. It took me 1 month to fully get onboard, to start promoting code conventions, culture, and actively participate in improving it.

  5. How does he take feedback?

    I love feedback, I'm happy to learn that I'm wrong and I'm actively looking for feedback myself.

    On one of my previous jobs I had a one to one and got feedback that I need to be a more reliable team mate so that people are calm to skip manual testing of my PRs, because they are sure that I did my best. I still have it written down and use each PR as a chance to make people feel safe about my code.

  6. How does he give feedback?

    People say I'm pretty good at it :)

  7. Will he contribute to the things outside of his direct tasks?

    GENERAL POINT ABOUT COMPANY SUCCESS

    A few cool things I did on previous jobs:

    • Orginized a knowledge-sharing meetup at a local cafe.
    • Writted down most of the team's decisions and outcomes of them.
    • Leveraged "Why we do this?" question.

  8. How does he operate in low-certainty environment?

    No one has all answers. If all details are figured out already - problem is already solved and I'm not needed there.

    My last job is a low-certainty environment, because it's a startup. We had to operate with low amount of info, make very high-level decisions and figure out together.

  9. Is he code-oriented or problem-oriented?

    I love solving problems. Code is just one way to sovle it. Honestly, the less code is required to solve a problem - the happier I am, because it's less to maintain

    An example when understanding of the environment and the problem helped me to write less code and move on to the next problem faster:

    We needed to dynamically identify meta data to a piece of content. Think of it as tags for articles. The proposed solution was to research for a 3rd party service which would give us a DB of tags and give set of tags per article. I suggested we just create a hardcoded table of tags and whenever we don't have a tag for article we mark it in the DB and manually process (create new tag and assign to acricle) which took much less time and worked great for a startup without clients.

  11. Can we rely on his honest opinion during a crisis?

    When I work on a project I think of it as my own project that I do with my partners. Of course I would address a risk for my own project to my partners.
